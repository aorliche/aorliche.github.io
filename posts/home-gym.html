I've decided that getting my old job as a tutor/computer information technology instructor at CCAC is taking too long (which is unfortunate as I really liked that job). Now that I'm no longer at a university like Tulane or Yale, I no longer have access to a university gym. Part of the reason I really wanted to get re-hired by CCAC is to get access to their - admittedly modest - workout space at Boyce campus. However, I've decided that I've waited long enough and that I might as well bite the bullet and acquire some workout equipment so I can stay in shape at home.

I'm really quite proud of what I've put together. From what I've seen online most home gyms easily run into the thousands of dollars. A really nice rack alone can cost you almost $3000. Now, I know not everyone is buying the cream of the crop, a.k.a Rogue, but the prices can go astronomical very quickly. For myself, I've put together a small space that can work almost every muscle group for only about $800 total.

As can probably be guessed from the photoes, my home gym is an Amazon special. I have their Prime membership as well as a Prime visa that gets me 5% cash back on purchases at Amazon. I also really like their policy on allowing returns on everything (which I've used once but not on any of the home gym purchases). Without further ado, here is the list of all my equipment:

  • Combo power rack with pullup bar, dip handles, J-cups, and safety arms for benching - $190.79 item link
  • Utilitarian bench for bench press, incline bench press, decline weighted sit-ups, incline dumbell curls, and triceps extension - $80.55 item link
  • Olympic barbell with 180 lbs. in bumper plates (total load 225 lbs.) - $240.56 item link
  • 25 lb. dumbells - ~$50 (I've had these for years, purchased at Dick's I believe)
  • Mikolo leg extension, lying leg curl machine, plate loaded - $201.39 item link
  • DMoose belt for loading with weights for weighted pullups and dips (ordered years ago) - ~$40 item link

The best surprise value for the money has to be the bench. Not only does it let me bench press, but it has breathed new life into my 25 lb. dumbells, which were slightly too light for me now. I was doing 35 reps of bicep curls without going to failure, and 30 reps of overhead pressing when standing. This was not ideal because I became psychologically tired way before my muscles gave out. Now with the bench I can do incline bicep curls which fail in the 12-16 rep range. I can also do triceps extension/skullcrushers which also fail around the 12 rep range. I've actually felt sore in my triceps for the first time in years after a heavy arm day, thanks to the bench and the exercises it makes possible.

Bench value notwithstanding, the star of my home gym has to be the combo power rack. Once I got it and the bumper plates, I could immediately do heavy weighted dips and pullups. Whereas before I had no access to a dip-like movement and I could do up to 18 pullups, I can now overload these movement and hopefully make progress in weight and reps. The rack is also serviceable for bench press and hanging leg raises to work the core. It is slightly too short for me (I have to bend my knees when doing pullups) but choosing the extended rack would have meant hitting the ceiling in the basement.

I just received and built the leg machine today. It's fine - I don't know exactly what to think of it yet. It's a little wobbly. The leg extension must be massively loaded for me to feel a challenge (greater than 90 lbs for a single leg). The lying leg curl feels super heavy, although this is probably good because it means it's not a plate eater like the leg extension. I have to use it more to decide if it's just fine or I like it more than my first impression dictates. It was basically the only value option for me working my quads and hamstrings, since I can't squat productively due to ankle issues. Fun fact: the instructions claimed that two people were required for assembling the thing, but I did it all myself in an hour and a half.

Now that I've given you a tour of my home gym, I can give a quick rundown of my informal workout schedule. Every day I start the morning with 2 sets 45 lbs. weighted dips and pullups, followed by 2 sets 25 lbs. weighted dips and pullups, followed by 2 sets bodyweight dips and pullups. Today I was able to set a PR for dips, with 10 weighted 45 lb. dips and 8 weighted 45 lb. pullups. I am really hoping to progress on reps with pullups, but more on that later. I'm also transitioning to having my daily run take place in the morning, after my initial workout, before it gets too hot. Then, each day is either a chest/back day, arm day, or leg day.

  • For chest/back day, I instersperse pullups and bench press throughout the day, with hanging leg raises and decline weighted sit ups. This day is my main focus, since I want to improve my bench (I barely did 8x 135 lbs. yesterday) and pullups, so that I can maintain my ability to do muscle-ups (though I have nowhere to practice them currently).
  • For arm day, I pre-exhaust with barbell curls and overhead press, followed by incline bicep curls and lying triceps extension/skullcrushers. As I said previously, I got a nice soreness from the tricpes extensions on the bench.
  • For leg day, I hit the Mikolo leg extension/leg curl, and use the combo rack for weighted lunges (I can do about 20 with 135 lbs. on my back).

The one x-factor for me is deadlifts. I have enough weight to challenge myself; indeed, I have enough weight to hurt myself! Once I got the Olympic barbell and bumper plates I loaded the entire weight and did 5 sets of deadlifts, failing on the last set. I noticed my spinal erectors hurting, but thought it was fine. My spinal erectors always hurt after hard deadlift. Due to peculiarities in my biomechanics, specifically in lack of mobility in my left ankle, deadlifts always just basically hang off my spinal erectors. The next day, I decided to go for a semi-hard hill run. Big mistake. At the end of the run, I couldn't even stand up straight. My spinal erectors were fully contracted and on fire. They hurt like nothing else when walking around, and even twinged when I lay down on my side slightly curled up. Thankfully, after two days of not lifting and not running, my they mostly recovered. I also noticed in the mirror how thick my spinal erectors were, compared to the rest of me. My main worry/problem is how to program deadlifts, if they take so much out of me.

Besides working out, I am on a two-month long diet with the goal of hitting a target weight of 145 lbs. The lightest I have ever been as an adult has been around 143 lbs., back when I was at Tulane and running a half-marathon every Saturday morning. A month ago I weighed myself at 161.8 lbs.; yesterday I hit 151.7 lbs. after a run. At this rate I can maybe hit my target in another month. I am focussing on mainting a 500-1000 calorie deficit every day, and am counting calories from all the foods I eat. I've made a little graph of my calories and weight throughout the diet.

Anyway, now that I've built this home gym, the only task (besides job hunting and slimming down) is to progress on weight and reps. Wish me luck!
